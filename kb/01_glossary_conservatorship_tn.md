# Glossary: Words you will hear in conservatorship

### ABLE Account
A special savings account for people with disabilities.  
Money in this account grows tax‑free and usually does not hurt benefits like SSI or Medicaid.

### Age of majority
The age when the law treats someone as an adult.  
In Tennessee, this age is 18.

### Attorney ad litem
A lawyer the court chooses to speak for the person facing conservatorship when that person wants to fight or question the case.

### Autonomy
The power to make your own choices.  
Being in charge of your own life.

### Capacity
The ability to understand a choice, the risks and benefits, and what might happen next, and then make a decision.

### Competent
Able and fit to make a decision or do a legal act.

### Conservatorship
When a court gives someone the legal power to manage parts of another adult’s life, like money, property, or important decisions, because that adult cannot do it safely alone.

### Co‑conservators
Two people (for example, both parents) who share the conservator role and help manage the person’s affairs together.

### Developmental disability
A disability that starts in childhood or youth (before about age 21) and is expected to last a long time.  
It can affect learning, thinking, moving, or daily life skills.

### Durable Power of Attorney
A Power of Attorney that stays in effect even if the person who signed it later becomes unable to make decisions.

### Guardian ad litem
A lawyer the court chooses to look into the situation, protect the person’s rights, and give the judge a report and recommendation.

### Guardianship
In Tennessee, this usually describes the legal relationship between a child under 18 and an adult who has legal responsibility for them.  
In some other states, “guardianship” is used instead of “conservatorship” for adults.

### Healthcare directive / Living will
A paper where a person writes down what medical treatments they do or do not want if they later cannot speak for themselves.

### Incapacity
Not able to give informed consent or make certain decisions.

### Informed consent
Saying “yes” or “no” to something (like medical treatment) after:

- Knowing the choices.  
- Understanding the risks, benefits, and possible results.  
- Making a choice that a reasonable person might make in that situation.

### Intellectual disability
A disability that starts before age 18 and makes it harder to learn, understand information, and handle daily life.  
It may happen alone or with other physical or mental health conditions.

### Irrevocable
Cannot be undone or taken back.

### Limited conservatorship
A conservatorship where the court gives a conservator power only in certain areas.  
The person keeps control over the parts of life they can handle.

### Minor
A person who is not yet a legal adult.  
In Tennessee, this means under age 18.

### Power of Attorney (POA)
A paper where a mentally able person gives someone they trust the power to act for them in certain areas, like money or health decisions.

### Reasonable person
An imagined “average” careful person who uses common sense and good judgment to protect themselves and others.

### Representative payee
A person or organization chosen (for example, by Social Security) to receive and manage someone’s benefit checks when that person cannot manage the money alone.

### Revocable
Can be undone or taken back.

### Rights
Freedoms and protections that the law promises to a person, like the right to own property, sign contracts, or make medical choices.

### Special needs trust
A legal way to hold money or property for a person with a disability so that the money can help them without making them lose benefits like SSI or Medicaid.

### Standby fiduciary
A person the court names to step in temporarily with conservator‑like powers if the regular conservator cannot do the job for a short time.

### Successor conservator
A person the court chooses to take over as conservator if the first conservator dies, quits, or cannot serve anymore.

### Supported decision‑making
A way for a person with a disability to keep their right to decide while getting help from trusted people who explain options and risks.

### Surrogate
Someone who acts in the place of another person.

### Ward
The person the court is trying to protect in a conservatorship or guardianship case.

### Person with a disability
The main Tennessee term for the adult the court is trying to protect in a conservatorship case.  
This is the person whose rights and abilities the judge looks at when deciding if a conservator is needed.

### Respondent
Another common Tennessee term for the person with a disability in a conservatorship case.  
“Respondent” is the word used in court papers for the person the petition is about.

### Conservatee
A word some people use for the adult under conservatorship.  
Tennessee law and state guides more often say “person with a disability” or “respondent,” but families may still hear “conservatee” or “ward,” especially from lawyers or older materials.
